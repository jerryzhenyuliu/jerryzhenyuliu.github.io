Why does the secant method converge at the golden ratio?

I was working through a numerical-methods experiment comparing three
ways to solve $f(x) = 0$: bisection, Newton’s method, and the secant
method. I expected the textbook story — bisection is slow, Newton is
fast, secant is somewhere in between.

Then I measured it and the secant method’s convergence order came out
to about 1.618.

That’s the golden ratio. That’s $\varphi$. That’s the number from
Fibonacci and pentagons and sunflowers.

What is it doing in a root-finding algorithm?

The three methods

Bisection halves the interval each step — that’s linear convergence.
The error at step $n+1$ is roughly half the error at step $n$.

Newton’s method replaces the true function with its tangent line.
It’s the gold standard: under nice conditions its error squares
each step. The number of correct digits roughly doubles every
iteration. That’s quadratic convergence — order 2.

The secant method is “Newton’s method, but for people who don’t have
a formula for $f’(x)$”. It approximates the derivative using a finite
difference between the two most recent points. You’d think it’d be
somewhere between linear (order 1) and quadratic (order 2).

And it is. Specifically: it’s order $\varphi \approx 1.618$.

The measurement

Solving $\cos(x) = x$ starting from $x_0 = 0, x_1 = 1$. The true root
is about $0.7390851332$. Here’s the error at each secant step:

| step | $x$ | error $|e_n|$ |
|——|—–|—–|
| 1 | 0.6850734… | $5.4 \times 10^{-2}$ |
| 2 | 0.7362989… | $2.8 \times 10^{-3}$ |
| 3 | 0.7391112… | $2.6 \times 10^{-5}$ |
| 4 | 0.7390851352… | $2.0 \times 10^{-9}$ |
| 5 | 0.7390851332… | $5.6 \times 10^{-14}$ |
| 6 | 0.7390851332… | $< 10^{-15}$ |

Watch what happens to the number of correct digits per step:

  • step 1 → 2: goes from ~1 to ~3 digits (gained ~2)
  • step 2 → 3: goes from ~3 to ~5 digits (gained ~2)
  • step 3 → 4: goes from ~5 to ~9 digits (gained ~4)
  • step 4 → 5: goes from ~9 to ~14 digits (gained ~5)

Compare to Newton’s on the same problem, which doubles the digit
count each step; and bisection, which adds about 0.3 digits per
step. Secant lives in between. But not in the middle — it pulls toward
Newton, which is where the golden ratio comes from.

The dashed line is the prediction $|e_{n+1}| \sim C \cdot |e_n|^{\varphi}$,
and the measurement tracks it closely.

Where $\varphi$ comes from

Here’s the reasoning. For a smooth function $f$ with a simple root $r$,
you can show (with some Taylor-expansion algebra) that the secant-method
error satisfies, in the limit:

$$
|e_{n+1}| \approx C \cdot |e_n| \cdot |e_{n-1}|
$$

That is: the new error depends on the previous two errors multiplied
together. (Newton’s method doesn’t have this coupling — each step only
depends on the current error.)

Now suppose $|e_n| \sim K \cdot \alpha^n$ for some constants $K$ and $\alpha$.
Substituting:

$$
K \alpha^{n+1} = C \cdot K \alpha^n \cdot K \alpha^{n-1}
$$

Dividing by $K \alpha^{n-1}$:

$$
\alpha^2 = C K \alpha^0 = CK
$$

Hmm, that gave me a constant, not an equation in $\alpha$. Let me try a
more flexible ansatz: $|e_n| \sim K^{\varphi^n}$, which says the error
shrinks “super-exponentially” in $n$, with some exponent that grows. Take
the log of both sides of $|e_{n+1}| \sim |e_n| \cdot |e_{n-1}|$:

$$
\log |e_{n+1}| \sim \log |e_n| + \log |e_{n-1}|
$$

So if I let $L_n = -\log |e_n|$ (positive when the error is small), then:

$$
L_{n+1} = L_n + L_{n-1}
$$

That’s the Fibonacci recurrence. And the asymptotic growth rate of
Fibonacci numbers is $\varphi = (1 + \sqrt{5})/2$. So:

$$
L_{n+1} \sim \varphi \cdot L_n
$$

which translates back to:

$$
|e_{n+1}| \sim |e_n|^{\varphi}
$$

Convergence order = $\varphi$. The secant method inherits its
convergence rate directly from the Fibonacci recurrence.

Why this is the thing I remember most from this project

I’ve seen $\varphi$ in dozens of places. Pentagons. Sunflower seeds.
Fibonacci ratios. Self-similar pineapples. The Parthenon, if you
believe the aesthetics people.

Every one of those is a structural appearance of $\varphi$ — it’s
there because the geometry or the recurrence enforces it. But this was
the first time I’d seen it emerge from an error analysis — as a
consequence of which two previous errors the algorithm depends on, and
nothing else. No geometry. Nothing you could draw as a spiral. Just a
recurrence.

The same number that describes how leaves arrange on a stem describes
how fast the secant method tracks down a root of $\cos(x) = x$. That’s
a lot more surprising than any of the usual $\varphi$ encounters.

Practical corollary

So which root-finder to use in practice?

  • Bisection: slow (~40 steps to hit $10^{-12}$), but cannot fail
    as long as $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ have opposite signs. Use it when you
    care about robustness more than speed.
  • Newton: fastest (~6 steps), but needs $f’(x)$ explicitly, and can
    diverge or land on the wrong root if your initial guess is poor.
  • Secant: ~8 steps, no derivative needed. Its robustness is between
    the other two. In most engineering code you’ll see secant-like methods
    (Brent’s method is a hybrid) chosen over Newton precisely because
    computing a derivative is often impractical.

And next time someone asks me “where does the golden ratio show up”, I
have one more answer.
