A median-of-5 pivot for quicksort — the result I'm most proud of

The assignment for my sorting project was to implement and benchmark six
classical sorting algorithms. I did. But part-way through, I got
curious about something: random-pivot quicksort works well on average,
but every single run is a roll of the dice. Can I do better without
giving up the simplicity?

This post is about the thing I tried, and the result (~18% fewer
comparisons, ~12% faster wall time on random input). It’s my favorite
outcome from the whole project.

The problem with a random pivot

Quicksort’s speed depends almost entirely on one thing: how balanced the
partition is. If the pivot happens to land near the median of the
subarray, you split it roughly in half and the recursion is O(log n) deep.
If the pivot lands near the extreme, you get a 1-vs-(n-1) split, the
recursion is O(n) deep, and the whole thing degrades to O(n²).

Random pivot is a pragmatic defense — it prevents an adversarial input
from being a worst case, but any single run can still get unlucky.

On average this is great. On any specific input, there’s variance.

The idea: sample more, pick the middle

Median-of-three is the textbook fix — pick the first, middle, and last
elements, take the median of those three as the pivot. It’s cheap (a
few extra comparisons per call) and noticeably more stable than random.

I wanted to see how far this goes. What if I sampled five positions
instead?

The five positions are evenly spaced across the subarray. I sort those
five by value, take the one with the median value, and use its position
as the pivot.

The cost per call is constant — five reads plus sorting five items,
which is some fixed number of comparisons. The asymptotic complexity
is still O(n log n); I’m not going to change that. But the split
quality improves, and that compounds at every level of the recursion.

The measurement

Uniform random input, same seed, single run (not averaged over many —
this is what the first experimental look felt like):

n random pivot 3-way pivot median-of-5
10 0.06 ms, 25 cmps 0.08 ms, 22 cmps 0.06 ms, 21 cmps
100 1.68 ms, 667 cmps 1.11 ms, 655 cmps 1.25 ms, 550 cmps
1,000 11.96 ms, 11,140 cmps 14.03 ms, 11,266 cmps 9.48 ms, 8,762 cmps
10,000 132.2 ms, 154,674 cmps 148.8 ms, 154,144 cmps 127.1 ms, 125,576 cmps
100,000 1394 ms, 2,016,270 cmps 1566 ms, 1,921,818 cmps 1230 ms, 1,641,453 cmps

At n = 100,000, median-of-5 does 19% fewer comparisons than random
pivot (2.02M → 1.64M) and finishes ~12% faster in wall time
(1394 ms → 1230 ms).

The gap actually widens as n grows — because each recursion level’s
savings compound with every other level.

What I think happened

Math says: if I pick 5 random samples from an input and take their
median, the pivot’s expected deviation from the true median is
substantially smaller than with 1 or 3 samples. The variance of the
sample median shrinks with more samples.

Concretely: a random pivot has ~1/n probability of being the worst
possible choice. Median-of-5 has ~(1/n)⁵ probability of all five samples
being bad simultaneously. The tail of worst cases gets dramatically
thinner even though the constant cost per call doesn’t change.

So what I’m buying is lower variance, not a faster asymptotic. That’s
reflected in the results: median-of-5 doesn’t beat random pivot by a
lot, but it beats it consistently, and it rules out the rare disaster.

What the real libraries do

After I was proud of this, I looked up what CPython actually does. List
sort is Timsort, which isn’t quicksort at all — it’s a hybrid of merge
sort and insertion sort that exploits pre-existing order in real data.
So Python doesn’t face the quicksort-pivot problem.

But Java’s Arrays.sort for primitives is dual-pivot quicksort —
picks two pivots per call, and partitions into three regions
(less, between, greater). The idea is the same: spend a bit more per
call to get more balanced splits.

So my median-of-5 is the amateur version of a real production technique.
The core pattern — “pay constant extra cost per call to reduce the tail
variance” — is the same.

Things I didn’t try (yet)

  • Introsort: run quicksort normally, and switch to heapsort if
    recursion depth exceeds log₂(n). That provably bounds the worst
    case at O(n log n), which my version doesn’t do.
  • Quickselect-based pivot: find the true median in O(n) time (there’s
    an algorithm). Too expensive for most subarrays, but might be worth it
    near the root of the recursion.
  • Timing against list.sort(): Python’s built-in will crush all of
    mine because it’s implemented in C. I haven’t yet — partly because it
    would be humbling and partly because the comparison only really makes
    sense at the algorithmic level, not the implementation level.

The part that surprised me

I expected median-of-5 to be “maybe slightly better” than random pivot.
19% fewer comparisons at n = 100,000 is more than I expected.

I also didn’t expect 3-way partition to be slower than random-pivot
quicksort on uniform random input — the yellow line in the chart above
is consistently above the red. 3-way is a big win on duplicate-heavy
input (see my previous post),
but on uniform random the extra partition bookkeeping costs more than
the infrequent duplicates save.

This is what I mean by wanting to see things rather than read about
them. “Algorithm A is better than algorithm B” is almost always true
only under specific conditions, and the conditions are usually more
important than the algorithm.
